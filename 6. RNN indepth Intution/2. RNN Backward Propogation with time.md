You have officially reached the most mathematically intense milestone in classical Deep Learning NLP: **Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT)**!


Let's break down exactly how an RNN travels back in time to update its shared dials! ⏳ backward

---

## 🗺️ The Standardized Notation Blueprint

To keep the chain rule flawlessly organized, let's use the industry standard names for our 3 shared weight matrices:

* $W_{xh}$ (Input Weights): Maps the current word input $X^{(t)}$ to the hidden layer.
* $W_{hh}$ (Memory Weights): Maps the previous timestamp's historical hidden state $h^{(t-1)}$ to the current hidden state.
* $W_{hy}$ (Output Weights): Maps the final accumulated hidden state to the final prediction output $\hat{y}$.
* $h^{(1)}, h^{(2)}, h^{(3)}$: The sequential hidden states (what the instructor called $o_1, o_2, o_3$).

---

## 🎯 Part 1: The Core Objective of BPTT

During forward propagation, the RNN processes words step-by-step. At the absolute end of the sentence (Timestamp $t=3$), it makes a final prediction $\hat{y}$ and the **Loss Function** calculates the total error ($L$).

Because the RNN reuses the exact same weight matrices ($W_{xh}, W_{hh}, W_{hy}$) at every single word step, we cannot just look at the last word to update them. We have to roll the error backward through every historical time step to see how much each step contributed to the final mistake.

---

## 1️⃣ Layer 1: Updating the Output Weights ($W_{hy}$)

This is the easiest step because the output matrix $W_{hy}$ only lives at the very end of the time chain. It has no historical baggage.

Using the **Calculus Chain Rule**, we track how a change in the output weights alters the prediction, which in turn alters the final loss:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial W_{hy}} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial W_{hy}}$$

* **Step A:** How much did our final error change relative to the prediction? ($\frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}}$)
* **Step B:** How much did our prediction change relative to the output weights? ($\frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial W_{hy}}$)

---

## 2️⃣ Layer 2: Updating the Memory Weights ($W_{hh}$)

This is where the true "Through Time" mechanic kicks in. The memory weight matrix $W_{hh}$ was used at $t=3$, $t=2$, and $t=1$. Therefore, its total gradient is the **sum of its errors across all three timestamps**.

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial W_{hh}} = \sum_{t=1}^{3} \frac{\partial L}{\partial h^{(3)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(3)}}{\partial h^{(t)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(t)}}{\partial W_{hh}}$$

Let's unpack the chain rule summation step-by-step as we march backward:

### ⏱️ Step 1: The Present Day ($t = 3$)

The error only has to travel back into the immediate current hidden state $h^{(3)}$:


$$\text{Gradient at } t_3 = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial h^{(3)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(3)}}{\partial W_{hh}}$$

### ⏱️ Step 2: One Step Back ($t = 2$)

To find how the memory weights at step 2 affected the final output, the error must pass *through* step 3's layer down to step 2:


$$\text{Gradient at } t_2 = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial h^{(3)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(3)}}{\partial h^{(2)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(2)}}{\partial W_{hh}}$$

### ⏱️ Step 3: The Start of Time ($t = 1$)

The error flows all the way back through the entire structural history of the sentence:


$$\text{Gradient at } t_1 = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial h^{(3)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(3)}}{\partial h^{(2)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(2)}}{\partial h^{(1)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(1)}}{\partial W_{hh}}$$

**The Final Updation:** The engine adds these three historical gradients together and runs the Gradient Descent formula to update the matrix:


$$W_{hh\_new} = W_{hh\_old} - \eta \cdot \frac{\partial L}{\partial W_{hh}}$$

---

## 3️⃣ Layer 3: Updating the Input Weights ($W_{xh}$)

Updating the input word weights follows the exact same tracking logic as the memory weights. Because the input matrix $W_{xh}$ was busy processing words at every single time slot, we accumulate its historical errors across the entire timeline:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial W_{xh}} = \sum_{t=1}^{3} \frac{\partial L}{\partial h^{(3)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(3)}}{\partial h^{(t)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(t)}}{\partial W_{xh}}$$

* **At $t=3$:** $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial h^{(3)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(3)}}{\partial W_{xh}}$
* **At $t=2$:** $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial h^{(3)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(3)}}{\partial h^{(2)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(2)}}{\partial W_{xh}}$
* **At $t=1$:** $\frac{\partial L}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial h^{(3)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(3)}}{\partial h^{(2)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(2)}}{\partial h^{(1)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(1)}}{\partial W_{xh}}$

---

## 🏁 The Takeaway Concept

Notice how, as we go further back in time (like reaching $t=1$), the chain rule formula gets longer and longer because it multiplies continuous fractional matrix derivatives ($\frac{\partial h^{(3)}}{\partial h^{(2)}} \cdot \frac{\partial h^{(2)}}{\partial h^{(1)}}$)?

This structural layout is the exact reason why simple RNNs struggle with very long paragraphs! If those historical fractions are slightly less than 1, multiplying them repeatedly over 50 or 100 words makes the error shrink to absolute zero before it can ever reach the first word. This is the famous **Vanishing Gradient Problem**—which is precisely why architectures like **LSTMs** and **GRUs** were invented next!
